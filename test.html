ENOUGH Report: Building a Food Secure Tomorrow

Section 1: A letter from Jeff Simmons, Elanco President

What would a world without hunger and malnutrition be like? A world where no one worries about where the next meal will come from. A world where we are all nourished by safe, healthy, and affordable food.

We want to find out.

At this very moment, a quarter of the world’s people suffer from malnutrition.[1] Millions are going hungry, and millions more aren’t getting the nutrition they need to positively impact society.[2] This takes a toll on individuals, families, communities, and countries. It frays the fabric of our world, and will only get worse as the world’s population—expected to surpass 9 billion by 2050[3]—grows.

But it’s within our power to change that, and everyone has a role to play. Together, we can build a food-secure world, and we can do it with the tools and techniques that currently exist. The answers are here; we just need the courage and conviction to act on them.

That’s how ENOUGH was born. The ENOUGH Movement is a global community dedicated to finding practical solutions for a food-secure world. We’re the people—the farmers, consumers, businesses, governments, NGOs—who are going to solve the world’s most pressing problem. And working together is the only way we’re going to achieve it.

This report details how we currently view the food security problem and our ideas on how we can start solving it. But it’s also just the beginning. The ENOUGH Movement needs passionate advocates who can bring its message forward, people who are willing to speak out on the need for real solutions to a problem that touches every community, every country.

Together, we can make a difference.

Section 2: Introduction

Think about the last time you shopped for food. Did you buy a gallon of milk and a carton of eggs, or a bottle of soda and a sugary snack? Did you travel many miles to buy what you needed, or did you visit the local grocery store? Maybe you produced it yourself, or visited a fast food restaurant. Or, did you skip the store altogether because the options that you needed to feed your family were not available or affordable? Your answer may be simple, but it hints at a very complex issue: food security—having enough healthy, affordable, accessible food to eat—is not a norm across the globe.

The lack of food security is a problem every day, in every country around the world. It isn’t limited to remote areas or underdeveloped countries; it’s an issue in urban areas and farming communities, on another continent and outside your front door. Ensuring that healthy, nutritious food is available and accessible to individuals and families is something our global community must tackle—together. It’s time for everyone to have enough.

Consider these alarming facts:

1. By 2050, the world’s population will have reached nine billion, and the middle class is expected to increase by three billion.[4] 

2. There will be a 60% increase in demand for high-quality, nutritious foods.[5] 

3. And as we’re already overusing our planet's resources,[6] we must find solutions to produce more food with fewer resources.

The ENOUGH Movement envisions a food-secure future, one in which everyone has access to the foods that will keep them healthy and strong. A world in which all of our nine billion neighbors are nourished by quality, nutritious, sustainable, and affordable food. 25,000 people no longer die each day from hunger.[7] Where our resources are conserved and more human potential is realized. In which, our children grow up healthy, active, smart, and strong—able to build an even better world for their own children.

There are 795 million undernourished people in the world today[8] who are waiting and depending on a food-secure future. It will take hard work, commitment, and collaboration to get there; but by implementing practical solutions and working with partners around the globe, we believe that food security can become the norm in our world, not the exception.

Our Mission:

The ENOUGH Movement is a global community working to ensure everyone has access to nutritious, affordable food—today and in the coming decades.

Call-out Box: Food Security Glossary

Food security exists when all people at all times have physical, social, and economic access to sufficient, safe, and nutritious food which meets their dietary needs and food preferences for an active and healthy life.[9]

Hunger occurs when a person does not have enough to eat to meet energy requirements.[10]

Malnutrition is caused by inadequate, unbalanced, or excessive consumption of macronutrients and/or micronutrients, including undernutrition and overnutrition.[11]

Modern food production refers to farming methods that maximize the amount of production per unit (per acre or per animal) while conserving soil and water resources to meet the food demands of the U.S. and global populations both today and long into the future.[12] 

Sustainability creates and maintains the conditions under which humans and nature can exist in productive harmony, that permit fulfilling the social, economic, and other requirements of present and future generations.[13]

Trade barriers are government-imposed restraints on the flow of international goods or services.[14]

Food deserts are neighborhoods and rural areas with limited access to fresh fruit, vegetables, and other healthful whole foods, usually found in impoverished areas. This is largely due to a lack of grocery stores, farmers’ markets, and healthy food providers.[15]

 

The middle class is defined as the social group between the upper and working classes.[16] What it means to be middle class will vary by country and region, but it generally entails a higher standard of living and more disposable income.

Smallholder farmers own small plots of land on which they grow subsistence crops or small cash crops, relying almost exclusively on family labor.[17] 

Section 3: Creating a Food-Secure World

The Challenge:

In the next 35 years, you’ll have nine billion neighbors. Think about what this means for our planet:

The growing population: An additional two billion people will enter our global population.[18] That’s six times the population of the United States,[19] two and a half times the population of Europe,[20] or twice the population of Africa.[21] 

An increase in demand: We’re not just talking about adding more people, but more people with improved lives. Three billion people will rise from poverty to enter the global middle class,[22] and will improve their diets by increasing the variety and quality of the food they consume. While “middle class” definitions vary, simply stated, as this population segment grows, billions of people will be living better lives. As such, the Food and Agriculture Organization of the United Nations (FAO) predicts an estimated 60% increase in demand for animal-sourced foods such as meat, milk, and eggs.[23] 

Overusing our resources: We’re already using more resources than we can afford, as it currently takes one and a half years to regenerate the resources we consume annually.[24] On this course, by 2030, we’ll require double the planet’s resources to meet our annual consumption.[25]

The strain of population growth on our resources is already a reality in too many places around the world. On every continent, individuals and families are struggling with finding consistent, affordable access to nutritious foods. This lack of food security has major consequences—not just for human health, but for our economies and societies.

The Spectrum of Food Security:

A lack of food security is often associated with extreme hunger and malnutrition found too often in developing countries around the world. But in reality, it encompasses far more than hunger, and affects individuals and families in every community around the world. It could mean famine in Asia or a food desert in the United States. It could be the 10% of the EU population that is unable to afford a high-quality meal—incorporating meat, fish, chicken, or a vegetarian equivalent—every other day.[26] There’s likely not a day that goes by when you don’t come in direct contact with at least one facet of the spectrum.

Extreme malnutrition and death from hunger: 25,000 die each day due to hunger and its related diseases.[27]

Food intake consists of a few staples, and lack of animal protein in the diet affects cognitive, behavioral, and muscle development in children.[28]

Call-out Box: A Food-Secure World - What It Means

Access: Healthy, nutritious food is available everywhere in the world. Diets are varied and provide adequate nourishment.

Affordability: Cooking a meal using wholesome foods is more affordable than over-processed food that provides little nutrition. As the middle class grows, better food options become more affordable, not less.

Health: People have access to the right quantity and quality of calories and nutrients. There are fewer deaths from hunger, less chronic disease, and greater human potential is realized.

Resources: By emphasizing innovation and efficiency, we can freeze our production footprint and preserve resources for future generations.

Stability: Consistent access to affordable, quality food can help reduce political instability and conflict, and create societies that are less vulnerable in the face of humanitarian disasters.

The Solution:

The facts are clear: it’s simply unsustainable to continue down the path we’re currently on. It’s time to break the status quo. To change the way the world sustains, farms, and eats in 2050, we have to change the way we act today. To change the future for our children and our children’s children, we have to implement the solutions proven to make a difference—and the time is now.

There is no single answer to the question of how we make our world more food-secure—and we won’t all agree on the best ways forward. But we can find common ground. By working together as consumers, farmers, producers, policy-makers, thought-leaders, businesses, and activists, we can ensure a positive ending to this story.

Just as the lack of food security has multiple causes, there are also multiple solutions. The ENOUGH Movement focuses on four key pillars—Innovation, Choice, Access, and Nutrition—each offering modern approaches to tackling this global issue. At the heart of each pillar is collaboration: every member of society has an opportunity to make change happen, today. Together, these four pillars coupled with the support of global advocates will ignite a solution to food security around the world.

Section 4: Innovation

Innovation: Evolutions in technology and innovation enable more efficient and resource-conscious production of nutritious food.

Call-out Box: Quote

“Sustainable global food security is attainable if we have open minds on technology and focus on high productivity and efficiency. We cannot feed tomorrow’s world with yesterday’s technology.”

Aalt Dijkhuizen, Animal Health Economics PhD

The Netherlands Wageningen University & Research Centre

Current industry standards and practices simply aren’t productive enough to meet the expected 2050 agricultural demand,[29] and adding more animals—along with the additional resources necessary to feed, water, and shelter them— is not sustainable. Farmers can’t meet demand the way they’ve done in the past.

The answer isn’t more animals; it’s increasing efficiency by implementing innovative, modern farming techniques in more areas of the world. In the past 60 years, a wide range of innovations has allowed farmers to produce more without increasing the available resources. In fact, in the United States, output from agriculture has grown 250% using the same level of output.[30] 

Call-out Box: Fact

When 16 leading food security experts from seven countries were asked to rate potential solutions to address food availability, “the application of technology” was voted as second most important.[31]

There are success stories to learn from. Since 1972, the availability of chicken has tripled: from 2.6 oz per person to 8.6 oz.[32] Thanks to farming innovations such as nutritional advancement, genetic selection, and improved animal health and sustainability practices, farmers are raising more chickens that are healthier and more productive than in 1972.[33] 

But the battle isn’t over. As the population grows and incomes increase, demand for chicken will rise substantially.[34] If we continue down the path we’re currently on—adding more animals—we will need an additional 75 billion chickens to meet demand.[35] But by implementing innovation and efficient farming practices, we can reduce the number of chickens we’ll need along with the resources required to support them.[36] We can meet future demand with 99 billion chickens—that’s 24% fewer chickens and 20% less feed, land, and water.[37]

Call-out Box: Filling The Dairy Gap

The global recommended intake is two 8-oz glasses of milk per day,[38] and on average around the world, we have access to about one.[39] Based on our current production trends, 4.5 billion people won’t meet their nutritional needs by 2040.[40] But this can change. Today, one cow produces an average of 2 gallons of milk per day. But in leading areas, it’s more than 7 gallons a day. Researchers predict that we can have enough milk and freeze our economic footprint if every year, every cow increases her daily production just 4.75 oz. Many countries around the globe are already increasing at rates 3-4 times this through their use of innovation.[41]

The next step: implement these solutions across the globe. A lack of access to innovation, science, and productivity-enhancing methods has prevented regions like sub-Saharan Africa and South Asia from achieving their agricultural output potential.[42] With proper tools and investment needed to increase production, agricultural output from Africa could increase from $280 billion per year in 2010 to $880 billion in 2030.[43]

We know the same tools won’t work for everyone in the same way. By supporting local, culturally-appropriate innovation, the solutions that are implemented will truly be the most effective.

Call-out Box: An Innovation Example

Combining grass-grazing with automated milking

                                                

Autograssmilk uses information technology to solve a growing problem in dairy farming. With EU milk quotas due to be removed in 2015, many European dairy farmers are looking to expand production and are increasingly turning to automated milking systems.[44] Around half of all new installations are automated – a higher level in Europe than elsewhere.

                                                

In general, these systems improve productivity, milk quality and animal health, while saving farmers time. But the fast take-up is having the unintended consequence of reducing grass-grazing, since cows need to be in the stall to be milked. Less pasture- feeding, in turn, reduces milk quality and cow welfare, while increasing both the environmental footprint and feeding costs.

                                                

Can grass-grazing and automatic milking be combined for an affordable price? That is what Autograssmilk is trying to work out, developing and testing a variety of cow movement systems. One device electronically identifies where grass density is highest and a gate selectively allows tagged cows into the milking areas or new paddocks. A second project is testing virtual GPS-linked fences. Aside from the technical systems it is developing, Autograssmilk has focused attention on using digitisation to solve an issue of direct relevance to farmers and food security in Europe. And it has highlighted the need to ensure that technologies which bring benefits in some areas, don’t lose them in others.

Section 5: Choice

Choice: Consumers deserve the ability to choose the food that fits their budget, taste, and nutritional needs, while farmers ought to be able to choose the production methods that work best for them.

Choice for the Consumer:

In developed countries, consumers often have choices when it comes to their food supply; they may even have a broad selection of stores, products, brands, and price points to consider. But not everyone has the privilege of treating food as a comfort item or lifestyle choice.

The International Consumer Attitudes Study analyzed what’s most important to consumers when they purchase food.[45] 95% of shoppers value taste, cost, and nutrition most when they make purchases; another 4% with flexible budgets purchase food primarily based on lifestyle factors. What these groups have in common—unlike the remaining fringe, a small group that seeks to take choice away from the majority—is that they both want the right to choose.

Call-out Box: Fact

According to an October 2013 online study by Nielsen, eighty-five percent of global consumers said rising food prices will affect their choice of grocery products.[46]

Choice for the Farmer:

Farmers need the freedom to run their operations in the way that makes the most sense for their businesses and their customers. Farm productivity directly affects food access, diversity, and nutritional quality.[47] According to The Chicago Council on Global Affairs, “Making agriculture more productive raises incomes for farming families, which can increase access to nutrient-rich food.”[48]

For example, small farms produce 80% of the food consumed in developing nations[49] and are the custodians of 75% of all agricultural resources in the world.[50] Numbering over 500 million, these farms are far from few, supporting upwards of 2.5 billion people and driving our global economy.[51] 

Now ask yourself this: could we possibly deny these farmers the choice of products and practices that will help achieve a food-secure 2050?

Examples of Farmer and Consumer Choices:

  1. Antibiotics: When an animal becomes sick, the quality of our food declines, the quantity of safe, affordable, nutritious food decreases, and increased feed and water resources are required. Used responsibly and in parallel with good animal care practices, antibiotics enhance food safety, prevent disease and promote animal health, treat sick animals or animals that have been exposed to disease, and protect animal and human well-being.[52]

  1. Organic farming: Farmers need the freedom to run their operations, and consumers need the freedom to purchase foods that fit their price, taste, and nutritional needs. For some, this means turning to organic farming as their best solution.

  1. Animal welfare practices: The human-animal interactions in dairy cattle is a great example of the positive relationship between animal welfare and sustainability, which is a conscious choice within the farming community. By using behaviors that the cattle prefer, the animal benefits from reduced stress,[53] [54] [55] [56] the handler benefits from increased job safety[57] and ease,[58] [59] [60] [61] [62] and the environment benefits from efficient use of resources per unit of milk.[63] Additionally, there are economic benefits from fewer health costs[64] and balanced improvements in efficiency of milk production.[65]

Section 6: Access

Access: Food should move from places of plenty to places of need; eliminating international trade barriers can help ease access to affordable, nutritious food.

While adequate, nutritious, and affordable food is a key component of achieving food security, ensuring that food can reach susceptible populations is equally important. Eliminating barriers to the international movement of food is one of the most consequential ways to improve the lives of billions of people. In order to feed the world’s growing population, food must move from the most to the least productive areas.

According to the 2014 Global Harvest Initiative GAP Report, “The role of trade will be critical in closing the gap between areas of high food and agriculture demand and those areas able to supply more food, feed, fiber and fuel. Continual improvement in global and regional supply chains and greater harmonization of trade rules across nations will assure that all countries have access to the agricultural goods they need…”[66] 

The impact: when greater quantities of safe, nutritious food are available at affordable prices, consumer consumption increases. For example, since the North American Free Trade Agreement was taken into effect in 1994, Mexico’s per capita consumption has increased by almost 50%.[67] That means millions of people with improved diets and lives—all because of trade! Meanwhile, domestic production has increased, proving that imports can improve consumer access and affordability without harming the local industry. In fact, in most cases, the local producers increase efficiency, ultimately leading to increased local production growth.[68] 

Trade increases food availability and affordability by creating opportunity for farmers to enter larger markets, by integrating economies, and by moving food from the most to the least productive areas. Greater quantities of food availability lead to more affordable prices, allowing consumers to buy more of the food they need to feed their families an adequate, nutritious meal.

Call-out box: Quote

“The ability of food to flow across borders is an important mechanism for coping with the global food crisis. This will only become more important as the population grows, and more and more food will need to move from areas of surplus to those of deficit.”[69]

In times of crisis, trade is key:

More than 80% of the world’s least food-secure people live in areas prone to natural disasters.[70] When disaster strikes, they lose livestock, crops, food stocks, valuable agricultural inputs, and vital infrastructure, severely undermining their nation from producing its own food. In times of crisis, trade becomes even more critical to ensuring that affected residents receive the resources they need to survive.[71] 

Section 7: Nutrition

Nutrition: Food should meet more than simple caloric needs: it should provide the protein and nutrients that support growth and development.

Nutrition—providing or obtaining the food necessary for health and growth[72]—is a critical element of development. A lack of nutrition can be divided into two equally serious problems: hunger and malnutrition.

While progress has been made in reducing global hunger, there remain 795 million hungry people in the world today,[73] and according to the FAO, hunger is responsible for more deaths every year than AIDS, malaria, and tuberculosis combined.[74] 

But it’s more than just extreme hunger that’s a problem. Chronic undernourishment and malnutrition—a lack of sufficient and/or healthy food—affects nearly every country on earth and more than a quarter of the world’s population.[75] According to the World Health Organization (WHO), one out of three people in developing countries suffer from vitamin and nutrient deficiencies that cause stunting, blindness, anemia, reduced immunity and other potentially deadly conditions.[76]

The depth of hunger and malnutrition effects are truly global, causing serious health risks, undermining the success of national economies, and imperiling cultures and societies. For example, poor nutrition causes nearly half (45%) of deaths in children under five-years-old—3.1 million children each year[77]—and adults without access to adequate nutrition are less productive and more susceptible to infectious disease.[78]

Efforts to achieve food security have often focused simply on “filling bellies.” While this helps to address caloric insufficiencies, it fails to provide people with the balanced diets and range of nutrients needed to thrive. Food security isn’t just about providing hungry people with food. It’s about ensuring that people are nourished with the nutrients their bodies need.  

Protein: The Missing Ingredient

Protein, including animal protein, is a vital component of a healthy, nourished diet. In fact, the WHO recommends a minimum protein intake of 48-56 grams per day.[79] At present, there is a large gap in meeting that demand. This gap is particularly large in developing regions of the world, such as Northern and Sub-Saharan Africa and Asia, where protein consumption is the lowest in the world.[80] For example, the typical meal of a child living in poverty in Kenya is made up of corn flour-based bread and cabbage, resulting in a diet that is inadequate in key nutrients like iron, vitamin A, and iodine.[81] This has serious ramifications for their health and development and creates an unequal, tiered food system that fails our most vulnerable populations.

Compared to diets based only on carbohydrates and the incomplete proteins found in plant foods, diets containing meat, milk, and eggs offer more of the nutrients critical for proper growth and development—both physical and cognitive.[82] A landmark observational study in Kenya demonstrates that when children’s diets are supplemented with meat or milk, learning and resulting test scores improve.[83]

Call-out Box: The Role of Animal-Based Proteins and Amino Acids

Animal-based proteins provide the full complement of essential amino acids, which carry out many important functions: giving cells their structure, transporting and storing nutrients, and healing wounds and repairing tissue.[84] Additionally, animal source foods, such as beef and eggs, can assist in the absorption of iron and zinc from other foods.

Section 8: Conclusion

In just 35 years, we’ll have 9 billion neighbors and an expansive middle class; the global demand for animal-sourced foods such as meat, milk, and eggs will increase by 60%; and we will require more of our planet’s resources than we can afford. The facts are clear, and we can’t continue down the path we’re on to reach the goal of a food-secure 2050. But by implementing innovative farming techniques that increase efficiency, enabling both farmer and consumer choice, eliminating global trade barriers, and ensuring proper nutritional nourishment we can ensure a positive ending to this story.

The answers are here and the solutions exist. A food secure future is possible. It’s time to solve the greatest issue of our time—together.
